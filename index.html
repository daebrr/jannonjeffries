Flow Builder Adoption

Overview

Merchants spend the majority of their time creating and managing their SMS campaigns and automations. Flow Builder allows them to accomplish this more easily by introducing multi-message functionality within a single SMS campaign or automation.

Problem

There is limited functionality and support for the current tools merchants are accustomed to using; however, merchants are wary of change and have been slow to adopt Flow Builder—in turn, missing out on improved features that can help make their jobs easier.

Legacy vs. Flow Builder

The legacy SMS campaign experience (below left) uses outdated design system components and has limited functionality such as a single-message restriction and lack of branching capabilities. Meanwhile, the Flow Builder experience (below right) supports single-message use cases as well as more complex flows that include important functionality like A/B testing.

Research

  • We talked with Customer Success Managers and the main points of feedback were:
    • No easy way to A/B test messages in the legacy experience
    • No way to exclude specific customers from a segment on legacy experience
  • We also received feedback from merchants via a widget on the home page:
    • Roughly 70% of users find Flow Builder easy to use

Objectives

  • How might we...drive adoption of Flow Builder?
    • How might we...increase the number of messages merchants are sending?
  • How might we...help decrease the amount of time it takes to learn Flow Builder?

Process

As the lead product designer on the project, I interviewed merchants about their experience using Flow Builder. In addition to running customer interviews, I created a Pendo survey soliciting asynchronous feedback and utilized Amplitude to track Flow Builder usage. I also worked closely with Product and Engineering by conducting a workship to highlight opportunities for increasing adoption.

Enhancements

We made the following updates to the product in hopes of boosting Flow Builder usage:

  • Surface a “Try Flow Builder” option within the legacy SMS campaign and automation experiences
  • Add template option for SMS campaign and automation flows to make building flows easier
  • Create an option to convert legacy SMS automations to automation flows

The addition of a "Try Flow Builder" button added to the legacy experience

A step in the process for migrating legacy automations to Flow Builder

Objectives follow-up

The updates we made to the experience resulted in an increase of ~700% for Flow Builder usage. We also saw ~25% of merchants who interacted with the “Try Flow Builder” button continue to create a flow.

Next steps

In order to sunset the legacy experience, we are working towards product pairity between tthe legacy experience and Flow Builder.

House Rules

Overview

Homeowners set property rules travelers must agree to prior to booking. These rules encompass items like max occupancy, minimum renter age, allowance of pets, and several other rules.

Problem

Hosts cancel on travelers due to confusion surrounding house rules. Vrbo has over 13,000 cancellations annually because of house rules. The result is unhappy customers and a loss of revenue.

Original experience

There have been a lot of concerns surrounding the original house rules experience for both hosts and travelers. For example, one early study found travelers less inclined to book a property when custom notes were included in the house rules. (The outlined sections pictured above are areas for custom content.)

Research

  • User Feedback
    • There are less conversions when custom notes are included
  • UX hueristic evaluation*
    • Participants had difficulty saving rules
    • Participants found a lack of affordance for different UI inputs
  • Customer Advisory Board
    • Hosts have always been vocal about desiring more rules
  • Natural language processing
    • NLP was used to detect the most popular custom rules added by hosts

* A UX hueristic evaluation is an internal evaluation by subject matter experts applying the Nielsen Norman Group usability hueristics to an experience.

Objectives

  • How might we...disincentivize the use of custom content
  • How might we...reduce cancellations and declines
  • How might we...encourage completion of house rules

Process

As the lead product designer on the project, I attended weekly progress meetings with the product manager, technical project manager, marketing specialist, and content strategist. I also worked closely with the traveler designer to ensure there was consistency across the host and traveler experience. Lastly, I regularly shared my working designs at our host team design critique for transparency and feedback.

Above are some early iterations for a rule about pets

Testing

Our UX researcher performed a remote moderated study where 9 participants walked through a multi-step prototype. Our team was looking for feedback on:

  • How easily hosts could edit and save rules
  • If each rule provided enough options for hosts

Above is a brief look into the functioning prototype

Outcomes

  • 9/9 participants were able to edit and save their rules with no issue
  • Participants were excited to see the new rule options
  • Some rule content wasn't specific enough for participants
  • One participant happily stated, "The more you can answer in rules without getting questions, the better!"

New designs

After updating some of the content and syncing with the Engineering team about scope, we were able to push forward with an updated version of house rules.

A look at the new design

The new host design utilizes components from the Vrbo design system, allowing hosts to always have the save action within view. The content in the host view is also clearly represented in the traveler view as the host intended.

A comparison between an old rule and a new rule

The original rule included an "on/off" state as well as options for "yes" and "no". The new rule has only the "yes" and "no" options with other more specific inputs inspired by early user research.

Rule states

In order to provide the Engineers with clarity, each rule has been broken down into its different states. Using progressive disclosure, we only surface relevant content when the appropriate rule choice is selected.

Objectives follow-up

  • No amount of new structured rules will be sufficient to replace custom content for hosts with unique properties.
  • Since the release of the new house rules design, conversions have increased.
  • Because house rules have been added to the onboarding experience, 100% of new users are completing them.

Next steps

To improve the house rules experience even further, we plan to add more rules after implementing additional natural language processing for host custom rules and notes.

Cancel and change

Overview

Hosts are able to change or cancel a guest's reservation if the need arises.

Problem

When a host cancels on a guest, everyone loses. The guest is disappointed, the host's search rank falls, and Vrbo loses out on revenue. Host cancellations contribute to 10% of reported guest pain points and current limitations of the reservations cancel and change capabilities lead to millions of dollars in operational costs—not including the revenue lost for Vrbo and hosts.

Original experience

Canceling a reservation

The original cancel experience has led to a lack of trust between travelers and hosts. In this experience, hosts are not required to explain to the guest why they are canceling.

Changing a reservation

The original change experience doesn't allow a host to add a payment request or issue a refund. These actions can be taken immediately following the flow, but there is little direction for hosts about how to handle these situations.

Research

  • Data findings
    • Host cancellations make up 10% of guest overall pain points
    • Hosts tend to cancel on 0-2 days after booking or after 2 months
  • Previous usability study
    • Participants skipped reading large blocks of text
    • Participants were unclear if the guest would be notified of a reservation change
  • Natural language processing
    • 36,488 cancellation reasons marked as "Other" were analyzed
    • "Property sold" and "Guest requested" were the top two reasons in the "Other" category

Objectives

  • How might we...help hosts avoid cancellations?
  • How might we...offer guests peace of mind when they are canceled on?
  • How might we...allow more flexibility for reservation changes?

Process

  • As the lead product designer, I had weekly meetings with the Product Manager and Content Strategist on the project.
  • During the host design team's UX critique meeting, I would often share my work for feedback.
  • I researched competitor designs (like Airbnb and Booking.com).
  • I presented the most recent designs to internal stakeholder for additional feedback and suggestions.
  • The larger host design team held a design swarm where one of the flows explored was the host change flow.
    • We created artifacts such as how-might-we statements, core user flows, and sketches. (Pictured below)
    • At the end of the swarm, we created a prototype that included a flow for changing a reservation.

Testing

While we are currently working with the research team to test the newest cancellation and change flows, we have gained some insights from the swarm usability study:

  • Participants appreciated a certain level of automation when approving guest-initiated changes
  • Participants wanted to be notified of guest changes via several different communication channels
  • When changing a reservation, participants admitted to only changing one item (like dates or guest count) at a time

Above is a portion of the tested prototype that included changing a reservation

New designs

Though we are still waiting on the next round of testing to begin, the new designs are based off previous research and extensive feedback.

Canceling a reservation

The new design adds friction against canceling at the top of the panel. We've also added a new required step of submitting additional context for every cancellation reason.

For host cancellations, we want to require 100% refund for guests since it's not their fault for these kinds of cancellations.

Though we may not be able to prevent all cancellations, we can at least help prevent future cancellations by prompting hosts to sync their calendars and avoid double bookings. This coaching is specific to the reason the host is canceling on the guest.

Changing a reservation

The options to change a reservation are now surfaced with the option to cancel. This allows for more scalability as change functionality grows.

The new design gives hosts the option to view their calendar availability when choosing to change a reservation's dates. In the past, host have provided feedback concerning how they prefer to view their availability via the calendar.

We have also included the ability to add a payment request or issue a refund within the change flow. The option presented will be based on a host's settings, but hosts are still able to override the selection.

Next steps

While working on the vacation rental designs, we have also been working on designs for larger hosts like hotel owners. Our two products, vacation rentals and hotels, are headed towards convergence and the cancel/edit experiences lend themselves as an optimal place for consistency.

Decline coaching

Overview

Hosts can approve or decline a traveler's request to book their property. The decline flow allows hosts to explain why they can't host a guest.

Problem

Host declines lead to disappointed travelers and a loss of revenue. Vrbo loses out on millions of dollars annually due to host declines. If hosts had an easier way to keep their settings and calendars up-to-date, a bulk of these declines could be prevented.

Original experience

The initial decline experience provides friction for hosts—possibly too much with the red symbol and dramatic red text. This design attempts to scare the host out of declining, rather than persuading them to approve.

On the next step, hosts are bombarded with a confusing list of reasons. Once they select a reason, they are able to decline without any guidance on how to avoid missing out on future opportunities.

Research

  • User feedback
    • Hosts don't realize they can sync their Vrbo calendar with other calenders (like Airbnb)
  • UX hueristic evaluation*
    • Participants found the reasons for declining a reservation to be confusing
    • The color contrast for the decline reason groups didn't meet AA compliance for accessibility

* A UX hueristic evaluation is an internal evaluation by subject matter experts applying the Nielsen Norman Group usability hueristics to an experience.

Objectives

  • How might we...help hosts keep their settings up-to-date
  • How might we...increase booking acceptance rates
  • How might we...decrease booking decline rates

Process

As the lead product designer on this project, I worked with the Product Manager and Content Strategist. We worked closely with the Engineering team to ensure our ideas were feasible from a code standpoint and received thoughtful UX feedback from the engineers in turn. I also shared the work-in-progress designs with the larger design team to ensure a variety of feedback.

Above is a mapping of the old decline reasons to the newly proposed reasons

Above are different explorations prompting hosts to block their calendars if declining due to property unavailability

New designs

Based on user feedback, we were able to pare down the reasons for declining a reservation request. We also opted to add a step mid-flow to block a host's availability. Lastly, depending on the decline reason, we implemented coaching either mid-flow or post-flow to help hosts save requests or prevent future declines.

Testing

We used an A/B test to determine if the new designs had a positive impact on booking acceptance rates and a negative impact on booking decline rates.

Outcomes

After a few months, we were able to validate our designs and pushed the new experience live for all users.

Objectives follow-up

Since the new experience has been live for all users, we have seen the following changes:

  • Acceptance rate +2.3%
  • Decline rate -2.9%
  • The selection of the "Longer stay required" reason has drastically decreased. This is most likely due to the new design coaching hosts to edit the request instead of declining.

Next steps

Since implementing new decline reasons, we've learned that many hosts are now selecting "Dates booked on another platform" (22%) when declining a booking request. This tells us that many hosts that list their properties on multiple websites are still not syncing their calendars between platforms.

With the new designs, we are exposing more hosts to the calendar sync functionality, but we still have a ways to go. The next step forward is to simplify the calendar sync process by exploring automation through app permissions.

Inbox redesign

Overview

Hosts spend the majority of their time managing reservations and messaging guests via their inbox. The inbox page is the second most visited page on Vrbo.

Problem

The inbox is home to booking requests, guest messages, cancellations, and so much more. Therefore, it holds a lot of value. That being said, there is a lack of urgency with hosts responding to guests—especially when it comes to inquiries (messages without a booking request attached). 10% of Vrbo's revenue can be traced back to inquiries, but only 3% of inquiries turn into full-fledged reservations.

Original experience

The original design treats every message with the same sense of urgency. There is no indication of unread messages or actions a host needs to take on a specific reservation.

Research

  • UX hueristic evaluation*
    • Participants had difficulty differentiating between read and unread messages
    • Participants didn't understand how to filter or sort messages
    • The icon options provided no accompanying content explanation
  • Design swarm
    • While contributing to a larger design swarm, we were able to put early ideas in front of test participants
    • One idea was to incorporate colored badging for booking status
    • This idea was received well by all participants

* A UX hueristic evaluation is an internal evaluation by subject matter experts applying the Nielsen Norman Group usability hueristics to an experience.

Objectives

  • How might we...drive hosts to respond more quickly?
  • How might we...make filters more useful for hosts?
  • How might we...provide a mobile-first experience?

Process

During the aforementioned design swarm, our team was able to do a lot of competitive and analogus research pertaining to how other inbox-like designs were constructed. Utlizing the swarm artifacts, I worked as the lead UX designer on the inbox redesign. I regularly met with the Product Manager, Engineers, and the Content Strategist to convey progress and troubleshoot issues with working designs.

Above are several early iterations of a mobile inbox design

Testing

Our UX researcher performed a remote moderated study where 9 participants walked through a native prototype. We were looking for feedback on:

  • How easily hosts could find inquiries
  • How useful hosts found the new filter system
  • How hosts felt about taking action from an inbox-level
  • How hosts felt about using swipe and long press functionality on native

In the prototype pictured above, participants were able to send a payment request reminder from the inbox. Participants were also able to utilize native-specific functionality like swiping in order to perform certain tasks.

Outcomes

  • 9/9 participants were able to easily differentiate unread inquiries from other messages
  • 8/9 participants discovered filters at a glance
  • 8/9 participants found the booking status badges useful
  • Participants didn't find the swiping functionality to be intuitive
  • One participant said, “I like it. It's cleaner. It's more app-like. It's easier to use. More user friendly. Overeall, big improvement.”

New designs

A new experience with clearer booking statuses

The new design not only highlights the booking status better than the original experience, but depending on the booking status, inline coaching maybe present to help hosts act more quickly when they need to. We've also added the estimated payout to inquiries and booking requests to help incentivize action.

A mobile-first experience

We started designing the new mobile experience and worked up to desktop—leveraging native iOS components to make implementing more efficient for our Engineers.

Another way we hope to increase response and acceptance rates is by surfacing response and booking acceptance metrics to hosts. If a host's acceptance rate falls below a certain threshhold, we can coach them to do better.

Objectives follow-up

This experience hasn't been implemented yet, but stakeholders are expecting a noteworthy impact in response times and acceptance rates.

Next steps

  • In the future, we want to add the ability to cancel inquiries in order to urge for host action on inquiries.
  • Currently, there is another page very similar to the Inbox called the Reservations List. We are exploring ways to consolidate functionality and create a single experience.
